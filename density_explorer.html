Reading the two panels together

Same points, same minPts. The left canvas is what DBSCAN gives you for one eps. The right skyline is OPTICS's full density profile of the data — every possible eps is already in there. Drag the cut slider and the bottom canvas re-derives a DBSCAN-equivalent clustering with zero recomputation of neighborhoods.

core point

Has at least minPts neighbors within eps. These are the points dense clusters grow from.

border point

Not dense enough to be core itself, but within eps of a core point — pulled into that cluster.

noise

Neither. Unlike K-Means, DBSCAN and OPTICS are allowed to say "this point belongs to nothing."

reachability distance

Roughly: the smallest eps that would let you reach this point from the cluster being explored. Low and flat = deep inside a dense region.

why the skyline shape

Valleys = dense regions (clusters). Peaks between valleys = sparser regions you'd have to cross — natural cluster boundaries.

the "varying density" dataset

Try it: one tight cluster and one loose cluster. Any single DBSCAN eps either shreds the loose one or merges the tight one with noise. OPTICS's skyline shows both, at different valley depths, simultaneously.

→ Start on "blobs" to calibrate what a clean skyline looks like, then jump to "varying density" and try to find one eps slider value on the left that handles both clusters well. (You can't — that's the point.)
